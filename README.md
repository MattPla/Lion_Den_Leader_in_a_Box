## The Problem

**Lion year is when we win or lose families.**

A first-year family's decision to stay in Scouting is shaped almost entirely by whether those first meetings feel welcoming, organized, and fun — or chaotic, stressful, and unclear.

The default system is no system. Each new Lion leader is handed a rank handbook and told to figure out the rest. That means:

| The Leader's Challenge | What It Costs | What We Lose |
|---|---|---|
| No standard materials | Hours of prep for every meeting | Leader time and energy |
| No central supply system | Last-minute purchases and waste | Family money, parent trust |
| No shared model | Each leader reinvents everything | Consistency across the den |
| No burnout safety net | Good volunteers exit quietly | Experienced adults we need |

New leaders are asked to be program designers, supply managers, and logistics coordinators — all for an audience of kindergartners, on a Monday night, after a full workday. Most of them don't stay long.

**This program exists to change that.**

---

## What This Is

**Lion Den in a Box** is a turnkey program system designed to give brand-new Cub Scout den leaders everything they need to walk into their first meeting confident, prepared, and effective — without hours of research or prep.

It is **not a craft supply bin.** It is an integrated support system with four interdependent layers that together address every dimension of what makes first-year den leadership hard: materials, logistics, human support, and program feedback.

The goal: a new leader can check out the tote, read a one-page Quick Start Sheet, and run a great meeting. Den meetings run in a one-hour window (7:00–8:00 PM on Mondays). This system is built to fit that window — with zero Sunday night scrambling.

## The Four-Layer System

### 🧺 Layer 1 — The Tote

A rolling utility tote containing:
- 13 labeled adventure module bins, one per adventure
- A program guide binder with leader reference materials
- Reusable core supplies shared across adventures (flags, compasses, clipboards, magnifying glasses, etc.)
- QR survey panels mounted inside the lid for post-meeting data collection
- A laminated Tote Master Inventory Checklist for checkout verification

Leaders check the tote out ahead of time at their convenience — or their paired mentor brings it ready to go. Either way, the tote arrives at the meeting already configured for the adventure. No Monday night logistics. No supply runs.

---

### 📦 Layer 2 — The Adventure Modules

All 13 Lion program adventures in individual labeled bins. Each module contains:

- **Den Leader Guide (DLG)** — Full meeting script and facilitation instructions. Step-by-step, written for someone who has never done this before.
- **Quick Start Sheet (QSS)** — Laminated one-page reference card mounted inside the bin lid. Materials list, timeline, and top three leader tips at a glance.
- **Inventory Card (IC)** — Complete contents list for quartermaster auditing and restock tracking.
- **All consumables** — Pre-counted and pre-bagged for 20 Scouts. Nothing to source, cut, measure, or sort.
- **Adventure-specific support documents** — Observation sheets, heat tracking logs, coordination checklists, layout guides, and leaderboard sheets as needed per module.

Every bin is self-contained. Open it. Run the meeting. Return it.

---

### 🤝 Layer 3 — The Mentor Pairing Program

Every new Lion leader is matched with one experienced pack volunteer before the first meeting of the year.

**What the mentor does:**
- Attends every meeting alongside the leader — not to run it, but to be present and steady
- Checks in between meetings with a quick text or call
- Helps the leader use the tote correctly: checkout, reset, QR forms, flagging missing items
- Coaches the leader through each adventure from first checkout to final reset
- Submits a brief observation report at the end of the pilot

**Why this matters:**  
The tote handles logistics. The mentor handles confidence. A new leader surrounded by kindergartners at 7 PM needs both. Materials solve one half of the problem. A human who has done this before solves the other.

The mentor is also a second set of eyes for pilot data — present at every meeting, able to validate self-reported stress and engagement scores and flag any gaps in the tote.

---

### 📊 Layer 4 — The Feedback Loop

Three QR-code surveys are mounted inside the tote lid and completed in under two minutes after each meeting:

| Survey | Who Completes It | When | What It Tracks |
|---|---|---|---|
| **QR 1 — Leader Survey** | Den leader | Post-cleanup | Prep time, stress level, youth engagement (1–5 scale) |
| **QR 2 — Consumable Depletion Ledger** | Leader or mentor | During reset | Items used, items missing, restock flags |
| **QR 3 — Quartermaster Reset Audit** | Quartermaster | At tote return | Reusable item count, damage report, tote certification |

Every meeting generates clean, structured outcome data from day one. Six adventures × 20 Scouts = a dataset worth presenting to Council.

## Adventure Modules

### Required Adventures (1–6)

These six adventures make up the full required Lion rank. Every Lion den completes them.

| ID | Adventure | Description | Key Materials |
|---|---|---|---|
| **RRB001** | Bobcat | Scout Oath, Law, motto, and the promise every Lion makes on day one | Pre-bagged secret agent cards and activity sheets |
| **RRF002** | Fun on the Run | Physical fitness, coordination, and teamwork through active outdoor play | Complete 4-station agility circuit |
| **RRL003** | Lion's Roar | Community safety: 911 calls, Stop Drop and Roll, emergency response basics | Pre-written roleplay scripts and scenario cards |
| **RLP004** | Lion's Pride | Family heritage, identity, and what makes each Scout family unique | Brown paper roll, pre-cut leaf shapes, markers — full family tree in a bag |
| **RKJ005** | King of the Jungle | Character, citizenship, and pride in community through creative expression | Shield templates and flag materials pre-counted for 20 |
| **RML006** | Mountain Lion | Nature observation, living things, and environmental awareness | Plot layout guide, observation sheets, magnifiers |

## The Feedback Loop

The pilot generates structured outcome data after every single meeting — not just at the end of the year.

### The 60-Minute Meeting Cycle

```
MINUTES 0–20:   Gathering & Opening
                Distribute name tags, lead the Pledge,
                introduce the Shared Leadership rotation.

MINUTES 20–50:  Execute the Activity Module
                Open the pre-bagged adventure kit.
                Lead the hands-on activity per the DLG.

MINUTES 50–60:  Cleanup & Digital Reset
                Lead a 10-minute cleanup.
                Complete the three QR code surveys.
```

### Five Pilot Questions

The pilot is designed to answer five specific questions worth reporting to Council:

1. **Did leaders spend measurably less time preparing?**
2. **Did leader stress decline when using the box?**
3. **Did youth stay engaged and active for most of the meeting?**
4. **Can consumable use be forecasted and restocked predictably?**
5. **Do reusable items survive repeated checkouts?**

## The Pilot Program

### Six Phases

| Phase | Description |
|---|---|
| **01 — Design & Planning** | Finalize tote format, mentor pairing protocol, and QR survey structure. Recruit 1–2 new Lion den leaders. Brief mentors. Confirm Council survey alignment. |
| **02 — Build & Assemble** | Source all supplies. Assemble tote and all 13 module kits. Complete Onboarding Guide and Quick Start sheet. Run one dry test before launch. |
| **03 — Pilot Launch** | First meeting. QR surveys activated. Mentor begins weekly check-ins. Leader runs first required adventure. Post-meeting debrief with mentor and coordinator. |
| **04 — Active Pilot (Months 3–6)** | Leader runs all six required adventures plus selected electives. Surveys completed after every meeting. Tote audited after every checkout. Monthly data review. |
| **05 — Evaluation & Report** | Compile all survey data. Mentor submits final observation report. Leader completes end-of-pilot interview. One-page summary delivered to pack and Council within 30 days. |
| **06 — Expansion Recommendation** | Present findings. Recommend next step: pack library, district pilot, or council-level lending model — grounded in evidence, not optimism. |

| Metric | Target |

## The Scale Path

This is a phased proposal. The pilot generates the evidence that justifies any future expansion.

```
STEP 1 — PILOT (Now)
One tote. One den. ~20 Scouts. Six required adventures.
Digital feedback loop active from the first meeting.
Report presented to pack and Council at end of pilot.

        ↓

STEP 2 — PACK LIBRARY
One shared tote set. Multiple dens. Shared quartermaster.
Item coding, version tracking, and replenishment standardized.
Expansion built on pilot data, not assumptions.

        ↓

STEP 3 — DISTRICT / COUNCIL MODEL
Reservation system, deposit structure, and bulk purchasing plan.
Council owns the reusable layer. Packs pay replenishment only.
Shifts burden from every family solving it alone to a shared system.
```

## Fiscal Snapshot

| Item | Cost |
|---|---|
| First-year startup (reusable assets) | $955 |
| Annual consumables for 20 Scouts | $580 |
| **First-year total investment** | **$1,535** |
| Cost per Scout after startup (recurring) | $29/year |
| Projected per-Scout cost at council scale (80 Scouts, 3-year amortization) | ~$28/Scout |
| Estimated cost if every family buys independently | ~$99/Scout |

The real savings at council scale are not in buying cheaper crayons — they are in **pooling the reusable layer.** The flags, compasses, magnifying glasses, clipboards, and hula hoops that every Lion den needs but that every family currently buys once and discards. Shared ownership of that layer is where the cost curve drops significantly.

## Document Naming Convention

All documents in the system follow a strict naming convention to support consistency and usability across the full library.

### Prefixes

| Prefix | Document Type |
|---|---|
| `DLG` | Den Leader Guide |
| `QSS` | Quick Start Sheet |
| `IC` | Inventory Card |
| `CARD` | Quick-Reference Card |
| `CHECKLIST` | Step-by-step checklist |
| `GUIDE` | Extended reference guide |
| `LOG` | Tracking log |
| `SHEET` | Activity or tracking sheet |
| `TEMPLATES` | Reusable communication templates |

### Adventure ID Codes

| Range | Type |
|---|---|
| `RRB001` – `RML006` | Required rank adventures |
| `EBU007` – `ERJ020` | Elective adventures |
| `OAL012` – `OSL013` | Target sports |

### Example

```
DLG_-_RRB001_-_Adventure_in_a_Box_-_Bobcat.docx
QSS_-_EGG008_-_Quick_Start_Sheet.docx
IC_-_OAL012_-_Archery_Lion.docx
CHECKLIST_-_Post_Meeting_Reset.docx
CARD_-_When_Things_Go_Sideways.docx
```

Consistency in naming is a core design principle. Every document a leader touches should be instantly identifiable by its name alone.

## About This Project

**Lion Den in a Box** was developed as a Wood Badge Ticket project by **Matthew Pla**, pilot program coordinator, 2024–2025.

Wood Badge is the premier leadership development program in the Boy Scouts of America. A ticket is a set of SMART goals a participant commits to completing in service of Scouting after their Wood Badge course. This project represents one of five ticket goals, selected because it addresses a real gap in new leader support at the unit level — and because it has a clear path to scalability beyond the single pack where it was developed.

### Core Design Principles

1. **Designed for the tired parent.** Every document is written for someone with 10 minutes to prep, not a training manual reader. Plain language. No jargon. Clear action items.
2. **Consistency is usability.** Naming conventions, document structure, and module format are standardized across all 20 adventures so that leaders never have to figure out a new system.
3. **The mentor is not optional.** Materials solve the logistics half of the problem. The mentor solves the human half. Both layers are required for the system to work.
4. **Every meeting generates data.** The feedback loop is not an afterthought — it is built into the lid of the tote. Six adventures of clean outcome data is what earns a Council conversation about scaling.
5. **Equity by design.** The system works across income levels, family structures, and prior Scouting experience. Leaders who are new to everything should feel as supported as leaders who grew up in the program.
